Universal Horizon Address (UHA) — Descriptive Overview

The Universal Horizon Address (UHA) is a framework for encoding the location of any point within the observable universe in a way that is independent of language, culture, or terrestrial conventions. Unlike conventional coordinate systems, which require external context (e.g., Earth-based units, reference frames, or catalogues), the UHA is designed to be self-decoding. Any sufficiently advanced observer, regardless of origin, can recover the intended location directly from the record itself.

At its core, the UHA provides a portable, universal “address tag” for the cosmos. It allows a specific position to be written down, transmitted, or stored in a form that does not rely on human interpretation. Instead of assuming familiarity with maps, calendars, or Earth’s measurement systems, the UHA embeds its own calibration anchors (fundamental constants of physics) and specifies its location within the natural boundaries of the universe. This design ensures that the record is origin-free (not tied to Earth or to any one reference point), intent-agnostic (it does not require the recipient to know why the record was made), and reproducible (different observers will independently reconstruct the same location).

The UHA also provides a robust solution to the problem of interoperability across cosmological models. Because different cosmological priors can yield slightly different horizon sizes and scales, the UHA encodes its data in a canonical form that allows translation into any consistent model. This makes the address portable across theoretical frameworks while still being precise enough to reproduce exact results under the original encoding cosmology.

From a practical standpoint, the UHA functions as a cosmic coordinate passport. It can be attached to observational data, messages, or physical artifacts to specify where in the universe that data or object corresponds to. It is compact, checksum-protected, and extensible, meaning it can be transmitted over noisy channels, validated for integrity, and extended with additional metadata without breaking older versions.

A key strength of the UHA is its universality for communication. Because it encodes positions relative to fundamental physics and the natural limits of the observable universe, it can be understood by any observer capable of measuring the same constants. This property makes it a strong candidate for applications in long-term archiving, interstellar messaging, or multi-species collaboration: situations where assumptions about shared language, symbols, or measurement systems cannot be made.

Finally, the UHA is not limited to static points. Through optional extensions, it can encode directional neighborhoods or multi-vector fields of view, allowing a single address to carry information about both a location and its surrounding context. This makes it not only a tool for pinpointing “where something is,” but also for communicating a reproducible local geometry — a compact fingerprint of a region of space.
