I address each of the Editor's points in turn:

---

EDITOR ISSUE 1: "The use of the horizon-normalised coordinate is simply a
redefinition after which no properly-calculated observable result could
change."

RESPONSE: This misreading is addressed head-on in the revised Section 2.
The paper does NOT claim that a coordinate transformation changes any
physical observable. The claim is that the standard COMPARISON PROCEDURE
--- computing tension as (H0_SH0ES - H0_Planck)/sigma --- implicitly
assumes both H0 values were extracted within the same distance framework.
They were not. H0_SH0ES is the best-fit in a framework with Omega_m =
0.334 (Brout et al. 2022); H0_Planck in one with Omega_m = 0.315 (Planck
2020). Since the luminosity distance depends jointly on H0 and Omega_m,
the extracted H0 values absorb the framework difference. The xi coordinate
removes H0 from redshift-derived distances not by redefining H0 but by
projecting onto the subspace in which H0 is genuinely absent. The
distinction between "observable unchanged" and "comparison procedure
corrected" is now made explicit in Section 2 (final three paragraphs),
with reference to specific equations in Riess et al. 2022 (their Eq. 16-17)
and Planck 2020 (their Section 5.4, Table 2).

---

EDITOR ISSUE 2: "The author is implying specific errors in the existing
literature but does not point to exactly where they are (ideally, specific
equations in specific key papers)."

RESPONSE: Section 2 now identifies the exact equations in which the
incompatible Omega_m assumptions enter:
- SH0ES: Riess et al. 2022, Eq. 16-17 (q0 parameterisation, equivalent
  to Omega_m ~ 0.327) and Brout et al. 2022 (full joint fit, Omega_m =
  0.334)
- Planck: Planck 2020, Section 5.4 / Table 2 (Omega_m = 0.315, fixed
  within LCDM self-consistency)
The q0/Omega_m equivalence is derived explicitly (q0 = 3*Omega_m/2 - 1),
so the expansion history difference is not an assertion but a calculation.

---

EDITOR ISSUE 3: "The different results are listed in a table with no
indication of how they were computed, nor why the Cepheid uncertainty
has become much larger."

RESPONSE: Section 4 (new, Statistical Methodology) provides the complete
derivation chain: adaptive quadrature computation of dc (Eq. 7), the
artifact fraction formula (Eq. 9) with bootstrap uncertainty (10^4
iterations), and the explicit derivation of the per-object Cepheid H0
estimator (Eq. 10). The Cepheid sigma = 13.97 km/s/Mpc is the sample
standard deviation across 43 independently analyzed calibrators at
z ~ 0.001-0.01, where peculiar velocities of 300-600 km/s contribute
30-100 km/s/Mpc per object (Section 4.3). This is why the SH0ES
pipeline does not use this estimator directly. The two uncertainties
(13.97 vs 1.04 km/s/Mpc) are fundamentally different quantities and
are not comparable; this distinction is now explicit.

ADDITIONAL IMPROVEMENTS IN THIS VERSION:

- Section 7.3 (new): Explicit engagement with the inverse distance ladder
  literature. Aubourg et al. (2015) found mild (~2sigma) tension with 2013-era
  CDL values using H0 = 67.3 +/- 1.1 km/s/Mpc; their expansion history fit
  is model-independent but requires CMB constraints on omega_b and omega_cb
  to calibrate the sound horizon absolute scale. Aylor et al. (2019) reframe
  the tension as a 3.0-sigma sound horizon discordance between CDL-derived
  and LCDM-predicted r_s values, concluding the tension is real and points
  to pre-recombination physics. Both analyses are consistent with xi:
  xi addresses the comparison methodology; Aylor et al. address which
  physical parameter is discordant. The fraction of the apparent 5-sigma
  figure that constitutes a clean, well-characterised physical signal is
  at issue in both frameworks.

- Section 3: DESI w0wa robustness note added; residual H0 dependence in xi
  under DESI best-fit dark energy is ~0.2%, well within stated uncertainties.

- Section 6.1: DES Y3 Omega_m noted separately (0.289 central value,
  consistent within errors with DESI range but not identical).
