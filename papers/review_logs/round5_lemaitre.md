## I. On the Sound Horizon — What Does a 3σ Discordance in *r*s Truly Tell Us?

I must begin here, because it is where I feel most directly implicated. The acoustic sound horizon — that comoving distance which a pressure wave in the primordial plasma could travel from the beginning until recombination — is not merely a number. It is a fossil. It is the frozen record of physics that operated in the first 380,000 years of creation. When Aylor and his colleagues at Davis set out to compare the sound horizon as inferred from the classical distance ladder (CDL) with the sound horizon as predicted by ΛCDM fitted to the CMB, they were asking: does the universe remember its own infancy correctly?

What they found is sobering. From the vector index I have examined, Aylor et al. find that the CDL-inferred sound horizon and the ΛCDM-predicted sound horizon are *discordant* — not merely in the Hubble constant, but in *r*s itself. Their methodology is carefully constructed: they do not need to extrapolate to z = 0, thereby circumventing peculiar velocity complications, and they note their results are "at most only mildly dependent on cosmological model assumptions." This is the key virtue of the Aylor approach. They have found a tension at the level of 3.0σ in the *size* of the standard ruler.

Now, this manuscript claims that a tension in *r*s could arise from "late-time measurement methodology" rather than from pre-recombination physics. I find this claim imprecisely handled. One must distinguish carefully between two possibilities:

**The first possibility** is that the CDL-derived *r*s is wrong because the distance ladder itself is miscalibrated — Cepheid zero-points, supernova intrinsic scatter, peculiar flows. In this case, the tension is indeed a late-time measurement problem. The manuscript's ξ framework may well address this, and I would not dismiss it.

**The second possibility** is that *r*s itself is genuinely different from what ΛCDM predicts — meaning the physics before recombination is not correctly described by the standard model. This is what Aylor's framing strongly suggests. They write that they are comparing two independent determinations of the same physical quantity, and they find them discordant. If the sound horizon is truly smaller in nature than ΛCDM predicts, then we need new pre-recombination physics. The Hubble constant rises because H₀ and *r*s are coupled through the angular acoustic scale θ* which Planck measures with extraordinary precision.

The manuscript under review does not fully disentangle these two possibilities. It tends to collapse them into a single "methodology" category, when in fact they are physically distinct. A frame-mixing coordinate artifact would explain apparent H₀ discrepancies in the late universe; it would *not* explain a discordance in *r*s that is constructed precisely to be insensitive to local-universe assumptions. This is an insufficiently resolved tension — I use the word deliberately — within the paper's own argument.

## II. On the ~3% Discrepancy in Ωm — Does Matter "Disappear"?

The paper's central physical residual, after the coordinate-frame artifact is corrected, is a ~3% discrepancy in matter density: Ωm ≈ 0.315 from the CMB (Planck 2020 reports Ωm = 0.315 ± 0.007) versus Ωm ≈ 0.295 from late-universe measurements. The weak lensing surveys confirm this direction — Amon et al. 2022 find Ωm = 0.289⁺⁰·⁰³⁶₋₀.₀₅₆, and KiDS finds similar values below the Planck baseline.

The question posed to me is a beautiful one: does the universe *lose* matter density between recombination and today?

No. Of course not. Matter is conserved. What we are observing is one of two things, or both in combination.

**The first interpretation** is that our model of how the expansion rate evolves between recombination and today is slightly wrong. The relationship Ωm = ρm/(3H²/8πG) depends on both the matter density and the Hubble rate. If H(z) is systematically different from what ΛCDM predicts at intermediate redshifts — the very epoch that governs both the BAO distance scale and the growth of structure — then Ωm inferred from the late universe would be displaced from its true value. This is not matter disappearing; this is the model's expansion history being imprecise.

**The second interpretation** is that there is a genuine physical effect operating between recombination and today — a late-time modification to gravity, or a dynamical dark energy that alters the growth factor, or screening effects in structure formation that cause the lensing amplitude to differ from the clustering amplitude. The S₈ tension (Heymans 2021, Amon 2022) is not independent of this Ωm question; they are related projections of the same underlying discrepancy.

The manuscript correctly identifies that this ~3% Ωm gap is real and worth explaining. I credit the authors for this. But they present it as the *residual* after methodology is corrected, as though the methodology correction is primary and the physical effect is secondary. It may well be the reverse. The Ωm tension has been present in weak lensing data for over a decade, independently of any H₀ discussion. It did not arise from frame-mixing. The claim that it is a residual requires more careful argument than the paper provides.

## III. On Early Dark Energy — What Does Poulin Actually Claim?

Here I must be precise, because the manuscript's claim — that EDE's ~3-5% shift "requirement" is consistent with the ξ framework's ~3% residual — rests on a specific reading of Poulin et al. 2019.

From the vector-indexed content of that paper, I can verify the following. Poulin et al. propose a scalar field — an axion-like potential — that contributes a fraction f_EDE of the total energy budget near matter-radiation equality, at a critical redshift z_c ≳ 3000, then dilutes away faster than radiation. The mechanism is elegant and well-grounded in field theory. The EDE contribution temporarily raises H(z) just before recombination, thereby *shrinking* the sound horizon r_s, while the angular acoustic scale θ* — which is what Planck actually observes — remains approximately constant because the angular diameter distance to recombination adjusts in concert. The result is that H₀ must be larger to produce the same observed angles. Poulin finds that this mechanism can reconcile the CMB-inferred H₀ with the SH0ES value.

The indexed chunk from Poulin (chunk 10) confirms: their model "begins increasing the tension with ΛCDM from Planck to 4.4σ" — I read this carefully — they mean that *without* EDE, the tension was 3.8σ, and that the SH0ES update at the time of writing had pushed it to 4.4σ, which is precisely what motivates EDE as a resolution. Their best-fit EDE model recovers H₀ consistent with SH0ES.

Now, the manuscript claims that EDE closes "50-70% of the H₀ gap" and that the ξ framework's ~3% Ωm residual is "consistent with EDE's ~3-5% shift requirement." This claim deserves scrutiny. EDE does not primarily *reduce* Ωm; it shifts H₀ upward by shrinking r_s. The "3-5% shift" to which the manuscript refers is the shift in H₀ that EDE can produce, not directly a shift in Ωm. These are related through the CMB parameter degeneracy structure — raising H₀ in EDE scenarios tends to push Ωm in the direction of the Planck value, not away from it. Indeed, this is a known *tension* in EDE models: they tend to worsen the S₈ tension even as they relieve the H₀ tension, because they require more matter to compensate.

The manuscript's claimed alignment between its ~3% Ωm residual and EDE's predictions is therefore, at best, qualitatively motivated and at worst confused about what EDE actually predicts for Ωm. I cannot confirm from the Poulin paper that EDE predicts a ~3% downward shift in late-universe Ωm. The consistency claimed in the paper is asserted rather than demonstrated.

## IV. On the Methodology Claim — Can 5σ Be Mostly Coordinate Artifact?

I wish to speak plainly here, as someone who has thought about these matters since before most of the authors were born.

When I derived the relation between recessional velocity and distance from the solutions to Einstein's equations in 1927, I was not doing methodology. I was describing a physical fact about the geometry of spacetime. The expansion of the universe is not a coordinate choice — it is coordinate-independent. The comoving distance to a galaxy, the angular diameter distance to the surface of last scattering, the luminosity distance to a supernova — these are all physical observables that exist independently of any particular coordinate chart.

A "frame-mixing systematic" — if I understand the ξ framework correctly — arises when one conflates comoving and proper distances, or when one applies a distance measure appropriate to one epoch to data from another epoch. This is a legitimate technical concern. Astronomers *have* historically confused reference frames. The early confusion between the heliocentric and geocentric frames in Cepheid calibration, the K-correction applied improperly at high redshift, the peculiar velocity corrections that Brout et al. 2022 discuss carefully — these are real effects.

But a 5σ discrepancy? That is 5 sigma. In modern precision cosmology, with Planck having measured the CMB to sub-percent accuracy, with Riess et al. 2022 building a three-rung distance ladder with geometric anchors verified by masers, parallaxes, and eclipsing binaries — for the discrepancy to be 93.3% methodological would require systematic errors that have escaped detection by independent groups using independent methods for more than a decade. The SH0ES team has checked and rechecked. JWST has now confirmed the Cepheid observations (Riess et al. 2023 find no significant crowding or confusion bias). Brout et al. 2022 achieve H₀ = 73.04 ± 1.04 km/s/Mpc with a carefully constructed covariance matrix accounting for all known systematics.

I do not say the methodology is perfect. I say that 93.3% is a precise number that requires precise justification. The paper provides a framework — ξ — and derives this percentage from within that framework. But a framework's internal consistency does not establish its correspondence with reality. The authors must show that the ξ coordinate correction, when applied to actual data, produces residuals consistent with their claim. I do not find this demonstration in the abstract provided to me.

What I do find plausible is this: *some* fraction of the Hubble tension is methodological, probably in the range of 30-50%, arising from the transition between distance regimes, peculiar flow corrections, and the calibration of the distance ladder. That the fraction is 93.3% is a strong claim requiring strong evidence.

## V. Overall Judgment — Does This Paper Deserve Publication?

I shall be direct, as is appropriate for a scientific review.

**The paper makes a genuine contribution** in identifying that the Hubble tension, when decomposed, has a component attributable to how different measurements access different physical epochs. The ξ framework, if correctly constructed, could be a useful tool for isolating the coordinate-dependent from the physically meaningful components of cosmological parameter discrepancies. The identification of a ~3% Ωm residual as the "true" physical tension is consistent with what weak lensing surveys have been finding independently, and this convergence is intellectually interesting.

**The paper has significant weaknesses** that must be addressed before publication.

First, the 93.3% figure is presented with a precision that the underlying argument does not support. The authors should provide uncertainty bounds on this percentage — what are the systematic uncertainties in the ξ correction itself? A number quoted to four significant figures from a novel framework should be accompanied by rigorous error propagation.

Second, the engagement with Aylor 2019 is incomplete. Aylor's discordance in *r*s is *not* explained by late-time coordinate artifacts — it is constructed to be independent of them. The paper must confront this directly. If the ξ framework truly resolves the Hubble tension, it must explain why two independent measurements of the same pre-recombination physical scale — the sound horizon — disagree by 3σ.

Third, the claim of consistency with Poulin 2019 and EDE is imprecise. EDE and the ξ framework are not measuring the same thing. EDE modifies pre-recombination physics to shift H₀; the ξ framework modifies late-time measurement methodology. These are complementary, not redundant, and they must not be conflated. The paper should state clearly: if ξ resolves 93% of the tension, then EDE is *not* needed, and the paper should explain why the Aylor *r*s discordance persists.

Fourth, there is a philosophical point that I feel compelled to raise. The universe does not care about our coordinate systems. If there is a tension between two measurements of a physical constant, the resolution lies either in our measurements or in our physics. This paper argues for the former. That is a valid scientific hypothesis. But it must be tested against the data with the same rigor that Riess, Freedman, and Planck have brought to their own work. A framework that corrects for frame-mixing should be applied to mock data with known H₀ and shown to recover it. It should be applied to the inverse distance ladder and shown to reproduce the BAO-derived H₀. Without these tests, we have a promising framework, not a demonstrated resolution.

**My recommendation:** Major revision. The theoretical framework is interesting and merits development. The conclusion — that the Hubble crisis is primarily methodological — is presented as established when it is at best a well-motivated hypothesis. The authors should reduce the precision of the 93.3% claim, provide uncertainty bounds, engage directly with the Aylor *r*s discordance, clarify the relationship to EDE, and provide quantitative tests against independent datasets.

The paper does not yet deserve publication in its current form. With revisions, it could become a valuable contribution — not the definitive resolution of the Hubble tension, but a careful analysis of its methodological components. That is itself a worthy scientific act.
